Chapter 07

Simple Machines ⚙️

Simple machines are all around us! They make hard work easier and have been used by humans for thousands of years.

⚙️ Let's Discover

What is a Simple Machine?

A simple machine is a device that makes work easier. It changes the direction or size of a force. There are 6 types of simple machines!

🪝

Lever

A lever is a rigid bar that rests on a pivot (fulcrum). Push one end down and the other goes up. See-saws, scissors, bottle openers and even your arm are levers! The further from the pivot you apply force, the less effort you need.

🛞

Wheel & Axle

A wheel and axle works like a rotating lever. The wheel (large circle) turns the axle (small cylinder). Cars, bicycles, steering wheels, door knobs and water taps use this simple machine to make turning easier.

🪜

Inclined Plane

An inclined plane is simply a ramp! Instead of lifting something straight up, you can push it along the ramp — using less force over a longer distance. Ramps, slides, wedges and even roads on hills are inclined planes.

⚙️

Pulley, Screw & Wedge

A pulley is a wheel with a rope — it changes the direction of force (like a flagpole). A screw is an inclined plane wrapped around a cylinder (like a bolt). A wedge is two inclined planes — used to split things apart (like an axe or knife).

💭
Think About It!

The ancient Egyptians built the pyramids using simple machines — mainly ramps (inclined planes) and levers! With no motors or electricity, they moved stone blocks weighing up to 80 tonnes. Simple machines are incredibly powerful tools! ⚙️

🪝

The Lever

Levers have three parts: the effort (where you push), the load (what you're moving) and the fulcrum (the pivot point). Moving the fulcrum changes how much effort you need. A long lever arm can multiply your force many times over — Archimedes said 'Give me a lever long enough and I shall move the world!'

🛞

Wheel & Axle

When you turn a large wheel, the small axle at its centre turns a shorter distance but with much greater force. This is why turning a large steering wheel is easier than turning a tiny knob connected directly to the steering. Cars, bicycles, and windmills all use this principle.

🪜

Inclined Plane

Rolling a barrel up a ramp requires less force than lifting it straight up, even though you travel a greater distance. This trade-off — less force but more distance — is the principle behind all inclined planes. Ramps, wedges, knife blades and even screws are all variations of the inclined plane.

⚙️

The Pulley

A single fixed pulley changes the direction of force — you pull DOWN to lift something UP. A movable pulley actually reduces the effort needed. Systems of multiple pulleys (called block and tackle) can let a child lift a car! Cranes, flagpoles and elevators all use pulleys.

🔩

Screw & Wedge

A screw converts rotational motion into linear (straight) motion. When you turn a screw, it digs into wood with great force. A wedge converts one downward force into two forces pushing outward — that's why an axe can split wood. Your teeth are wedges!

🤯 WOW! The ancient Egyptians used ramps to build the pyramids, and the Romans built roads at precise angles — but the greatest simple machine achievement might be the Antikythera mechanism (100 BC) — a complex system of 30+ gears that predicted astronomical events. It's the world's oldest known computer!
